Abstract

Current robotic evaluation is dominated by binary success rates, which collapse rich execution processes into a single outcome and obscure critical qualities such as progress, efficiency, and stability. To address this limitation, we propose PRM-as-a-Judge, a dense evaluation paradigm that leverages Process Reward Models (PRMs) to audit policy execution directly from trajectory videos, treating visual state evolution as a proxy for physical progress.

Central to this paradigm is the OPD (Outcome–Process–Diagnosis) metric system, which formalizes execution quality via a task-aligned progress potential. We show that any valid dense evaluator must satisfy two axiomatic properties: macro-consistency, ensuring additivity and path independence, and micro-resolution, enabling sensitivity to fine-grained physical evolution. We argue that PRMs constitute a uniquely suitable class of evaluators that satisfies both properties under the proposed formulation.

We empirically validate the micro-resolution property using RoboPulse, a diagnostic benchmark probing micro-scale progress discrimination, where PRMs significantly outperform discriminative and similarity-based alternatives, while macro-consistency follows from the induced global progress potential. Finally, leveraging PRM-as-a-Judge, we conduct a comprehensive audit of mainstream policy paradigms across long-horizon tasks, revealing behavioral signatures and failure modes that are invisible to outcome-only metrics.
